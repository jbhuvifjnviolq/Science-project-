Optical Fiber Basics

Principle of Total Internal Reflection

Optical fibers work by trapping light inside a thin glass core. This happens because of total internal reflection. When light travels through the core and strikes the core-cladding boundary at a steep angle (greater than the critical angle), it reflects completely back into the core instead of passing out. The core has a higher refractive index than the cladding, which causes this reflection. This way, the light continually bounces down the length of the fiber, allowing data signals to be transmitted over long distances quickly.

Composition of Fiber Optic Cables

A fiber optic cable is made of several layers, each with a specific function:

  • Core: A thin glass (or plastic) filament at the center that carries the light signals. The core has the highest refractive index.
  • Cladding: A glass layer surrounding the core with a slightly lower refractive index. This layer causes the light to reflect back into the core (total internal reflection).
  • Coating (Buffer): A protective plastic coating around the cladding that cushions the fiber and protects it from moisture and damage.
  • Strength Members: Fibers (such as Kevlar strands) that give the cable tensile strength and prevent the glass fiber from stretching or breaking.
  • Outer Jacket: The outer protective sheath made of plastic that shields the entire cable from the environment, abrasion, and crushing.

Fun Facts

  • Fiber optic cables transmit data as pulses of light, allowing extremely fast internet speeds (often in the gigabits per second).
  • They are incredibly thin (about the diameter of a human hair) and flexible, and can even be coiled tightly.
  • Fibers are non-conductive and immune to electromagnetic interference, so signals are clearer and more reliable.
  • The total length of all fiber optic cables around the world stretches for millions of kilometers, connecting continents for global communication.
  • Besides telecommunications, fiber optics are used in medicine (like endoscopes for internal imaging) and in various sensors (e.g., temperature, pressure).
